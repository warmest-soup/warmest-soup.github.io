To live is to know the internal experience, or perhaps its structure, which created all those who came before us, and all who will come after. The Human Experience is alike between individuals. Through the assumption of roles (or characters ) we can learn to better understand ourselves, and our peers with whom we share this life, it's beauties, and it's sorrows.

This is the purpose behind RATTRAPS.

RATTRAPS is a role play system designed with realism at the forefront. As such, all systems have been derived from their scientific and historical counterparts.
In service of this, The Rule of Argument supersedes all others. This is the principle that; If an argument can be made sound, the action may be allowed. The G.M. will have final say in determining what constitutes a sound argument, but if, using logical principles and historical president, a directing player can satisfactorily articulate that a certain feat should be possible. This will override any rule herein.
The purpose of RATTRAPS is ultimately the simulation of reality, for the purpose of exploring characters. Thus the rules are always subordinate to the logic that was used to create them.

This rule also helps as a directing player's balance against the fallible G.M. and helps to ensure a fair representation of their character.
"Nothing is impossible, not if you can dream it. That's what being a scientist is all about."
-Hubert J. Farnsworth
RATTRAPS is most commonly played via correspondence, with each directing player in isolation from one another. this preserves anonymity, and allows players to immerse themselves fully in their character's thought process and decision making style absent the social pressures of a party, or group.
Betrayers betray, thieves steal, and villains must commit villainy. The only punishment to be faced for realizing your character well, are to be dealt unto the character in the form of reactions from the word and it's inhabitants, including other characters directed by other players. Communities are policed, murders are investigated, and crimes are punished via a judicial systems and avengers.
One should not hold back from themselves for the sake of others.

Players will take turns with their G.M., with the players directing their characters in response to the developments provided from the G.M..
Players will be assisted in tracking their character's needs and abilities using their character sheet.

This forms the basic structure of the game.

Below will be listed, in detail, all the terms, and mechanics that will be used in the course of a run.
-General-
Game Manager: The player responsible for rendering the game world, and directing NPCs. This player also will narrate the unconscious thoughts of player characters. These thoughts will never affect their decision making or personality, but will give their directing players a better understanding of their character's physical and mental state. For example the remnant feelings of pain from a past injury, or the sensations caused by failed checks are within the realm of the game manager's control, not the directing player.

Direction: You are not your character. in RATTRAPS characters have their own skillsets & abilities that they'll have to use in order to solve their problems. These abilities are often times unequal to the directing player's own faculties, thus it is better to approach your character not from the mindset of "controlling" them, but rather you are "directing" their actions.
They are their own person, and you are aiding them along, immersing yourself in their life.

Player: There exist two kinds of players in RATTRAPS; One is the G.M. while the others all direct their own individual characters. The generic term players most often refers to the latter kind.
It is the job of these players to immerse themselves in the role of their characters, and embody their personalities when directing their actions.
Players are not expected to cooperate, or ally themselves with one another. In fact this behavior is discouraged if it would compromise the character's own way of thinking.
To this end it is recommended that players maintain their anonymity during a run. Not knowing what player is directing what character helps to ensure authentic interactions and prevents interpersonal tensions. You are not your characters.

Character: Each player will create their own character. These can be anyone, accountants, adventurers, soldiers, villains, royalty, anyone. These characters will be defined by their traits & abilities as well as by the personality that their directing player chooses to give them.
Players will direct their character until the run ends, or until the character retires, or dies.
These characters, and their interactions, are the backbone of RATTRAPS. It is through them that we can learn about ourselves, and the peers we share this life with.
Players will be aided in the management of their characters by their character sheets, which contain all manner of helpful & necessary functions.

Non-Player Characters: It is the G.M.'s responsibility to fill the game world with interesting "NPCs" for the main cast to interact with. These will include the local citizenry, law enforcement, rivals, allies, enemies, and any other necessary persons to give the world its depth.
During the character creation process, players may request that the G.M. direct NPC companions for their character. This can be a massive advantage, as well as a great enhancement to one's story through the run.
NPCs can also be generated at random through the Tarot system.

Cast: The cast refers collectively to all player characters. As these characters have the full creative attention of their directing players behind them, they will easily be the most interesting entities in any given run, as well as most likely to drive its plot forward.

Run: RATTRAPS centers around the interconnected stories of its Cast. There is no goal in RATTRAPS. Instead these stories and wander and weave in and out of each other as each character pursues their own individual goals.
Runs are primarily defined by the creative team behind them. This includes the G.M. as well as all the involved players.

Context: The term "Context" Is used to refer to advantages, bonuses, and modifiers derived from the relavant character's current situation.
For example, a Context Advantage is an advantage that exists only due to the character's current situation, such as having access to written instructions on how to perform a task, being assisted by a character with relavant knowledge, or having the correct tools for the job.

* The complexity of a task, whether something is easy or difficult from the relavant approach, is also a "contextual advantage" though these sorts of modifiers are solely the responsibility of the G.M.
-Mechanics-

Checks: Whenever a character's abilities are tested, a check is performed. The player will roll a pair of standard seven-sum dice. If the lower of the die is equal to or less than the check's bar, then the check succeeds granting a favorable outcome to performing character.
If both dice are greater than the bar, then the check fails granting the character a negative outcome.
Should both rolled die show the same number (A pair), the result is considered critical.

The bar for checks can be raised by advantages and will be lowered penalties. Unfavorable rolls can be adjusted using the focus mechanic.

Ability checks come in three forms;
Flat checks will ignore all penalties & advantages, and cannot be focused.
Passive Checks which will be rolled by the G.M. without informing the directing player. As such these cannot be focused either.
Active Checks Which are performed at will by directing players.

The G.M. will always inform players whenever a passive check has been performed on behalf of their character, but players will not be informed if any of their checks succeed or fail. This is to ensure authenticity in the character's reaction to their own attempts.

50-50: A less common style of check, used primarily by the G.M., in which a single dice is rolled with all odd numbers representing one option, and all even numbers representing a second.
These are most often used to make quick determinations in situations with two acceptable outcomes, and rarely will be used to make decisions of true importance.

Bar: The highest number a character can roll to pass an ability check. If the tested ability's rank is 3, the character must roll a 3 or below to pass.
The bar of checks is always modified by the total number of penalties & advantages applied to it.

Players will often need to estimate what the bar is for their checks based on the situation, and their character. One advantage will cancel out one penalty.

Advantages: Advantages are anything that makes an ability check easier for the performing character.
For example; were a character to attempt to leap across a gap in the floor, having footwear that grants extra traction would be an advantage in their favor. The floor being level and even, with no obstructions, would be another advantage.
Since each advantage raises the bar by 1 the hypothetical check above would have it's bar raised by 2, making it significantly easier to pass.

Advantages such as the ones used in the above example, that are derived from the current situation, are referred to as Contextual Advantages. These can be stacked with any incumbent advantages to greatly reduce the risk faced by the performing character, so be sure to always consider your situation carefully before rolling.

Every advantage on a passed check will decrease the exp earned for passing by 1.
In the event that an ability class or technique is used as an advantage in a check, experience lost will be awarded to the first class advantage noted.

Penalties: Penalties are anything that makes an ability check more difficult for the performing character.
For example; were a character to attempt reading a dense academic book, dim lighting would be a penalty. Were the character on a bumpy wagon ride, the erratic motions would be another penalty.
Since each penalty lowers the bar by 1 the hypothetical check above would have its bar decreased by 2, making it significantly more difficult to pass.

Penalties such as the ones used in the above example, that are derived from the current situation, are referred to as Contextual Penalties. These will be stacked with any incumbent penalties to greatly increase the difficulty faced by the performing character. Be sure to always consider your situation carefully before rolling.

The G.M. will not inform players of contextual penalties when they are performing a check.
Always exercise your own caution when directing characters.

Every penalty on a passed check will increase the exp earned for passing by 1.

Incumbents: Any trait or ability that's rank is not 3, has incumbent advantages if it's higher, and incumbent penalties if it's lower.

Rank 1: 2 penalties
Rank 2: 1 penalties.
⭡ ⭡ ⭡
Rank 3: No incumbents.
⭣ ⭣ ⭣
Rank 4: 1 advantages.
Rank 5: 2 advantages.


These are representative of your mastery (Or lack thereof) of the ability in question.
Incumbent advantages & penalties will still modify the experience earned from checks. Meaning it's more difficult to earn experience for high rank abilities, as they require more difficult checks to earn experience.

Incumbents are lost and earned naturally as a character progresses through ranks. all abilities are affected by incumbents.

Quirks: During character creation, when setting their trait ranks, Players can choose to replace incumbent advantages or penalties with positive or negative quirks related to the trait in question.
Penalties must be replaced with negative quirks, while penalties must be replaced with positive quirks.
Quirks are in essence, niche advantages & penalties, that are unlikely to affect all your checks, but due to their nature may affect checks for more than just the trait they're under.
For example; A character might choose arachnophobia as a Wisdom Quirk. They will no longer need to overcome their incumbent Penalty, but will have to deal with more severe penalties when around spiders, or things that remind them of spiders.

Quirks can be used to turn Penalties into advantages through their unintended side effects. Another example; A character might choose to take paranoia as a negative Perception Quirk. This might cause unnecessary checks, but would also grant advantages in the event of a real threat. More frequent checks would also lead to more experience gained.

Unlike incumbents, Quirks do not disappear as one raises their trait ranks. When an incumbent penalty is replaced with a negative quirk, that quirk is kept indefinitely. Whenever a character raises a Quirked trait rank, the directing player may choose not to raise their rank in order to remove their negative quirk.

Quirks can also be gained or lost rarely through developments in a character's personal story.

* Quirks are to be listed and briefly explained in the notes section of one's character sheet for the G.M.'s reference.

Seven-Sum Dice: RATTRAPS is played using two standard Seven-Sum Dice. This means that opposite faces will always add up to seven. (Five is opposite two, six is opposite one.)

Focus: Characters can modify any dice roll using the relevant trait. Dex is used to focus skill rolls, while Wisdom is used to focus insight rolls.

When focusing a roll, the either dice maybe raised or lowered by one, as many times as the performing character has ranks in the relevant trait.

Focusing a roll costs one card of relevant type.

* Focus cannot be used on any roll that resulted in a pair of 6s.

Rank: A character's talent in any given trait or class. Ranks determine the natural bar of checks which become easier as Ranks increase, as well as that ability's incumbents.
Trait Ranks also determine a character's access to quirks while technique class ranks often provide class bonuses that grow in effectiveness with higher ranks.

Traits: The fundamental abilities that define a character. Traits are generic, and widely applicable abilities. Like all abilities, they receive incumbents, but unlike skills & insights, the incumbents of traits can be traded for Quirks to further specialize a character.
-Traits-
Athleticism: A category of traits that, together, offer an idea of a character's aptitude for athletics. These are the primary traits used in combat, but affect all physical fields as well.
Athleticism includes Speed, Strength, and Dexterity.

Strength: A measure of a character's ability to move and carry weight. Strength determines how much of a load they can carry, what objects they can lift, and what weapons they have difficulty wielding.
Strength is greatly beneficial in all manners of work and labor, as well as in grappling exchanges, where strength is added to their grapple check, making foes easier to deal with.

Dexterity: A measure of a character's fine motor skills. Dexterity determines their effectiveness with edged weapons, how likely they are to be disarmed, as well as how many actions they're able to perform in one round of combat.
Dexterity is extremely useful for finer actions, such as playing instruments, writing, and Precision striking.

Dexterity is also the trait used to focus skill rolls and checks.

Speed: A measure of a character's gross motor skills and coordination. Speed determines they melee damage, How likely they are to recover from being destabilized, their resistance to being tripped, as well as how many actionable moments they have in every round of combat.
When it comes to combat, Speed is the most important trait.

Athleticism and its subordinate traits are considered to be skills.
Awareness: A category of traits that, together, offer an idea of a character's understanding of the things around them. These are the primary traits used in exploration, and social situations.
Awareness includes Perception, Navigation, and Charisma.

Awareness and it's subordinate traits are considered to be insights.

Perception: A measure of a character's situational awareness and the acuity of their sense. Perception determines how much detail they can perceive in their environment, how easy it is to visually target objects & entities with ranged weapons & magic, and how much of their battle ticket *must* be turned in at a time.
For cautious characters, or those interested in gathering detail from the environment, perception is very important.

Navigation: A measure of a character's spacial awareness and their ease of orienting themselves in space. Navigation determines a character's ability to avoid getting lost or turned around, and is very important when traveling often or navigating dense interior spaces.

Charisma: A measure of a character's social awareness, and their ability to understand the social cues & meanings behind the words of others. For social characters, charisma is the most important trait as it allows a greater understanding of the emotional states & motivations of the character's one interacts with.

Body: A category of traits that, together, offer an idea of a character's physical hardiness. These are the primary traits used in determining one's physical wellness, and one's natural resistances.
Body includes Endurance, Constitution, and Stamina.

Body and its subordinate traits are considered to be Skills

Stamina: A measure of a character's physical fitness, and how well they can ignore physical discomfort. Stamina primarily determines the maximum number of skill cards a character has access to, and thus, how often they can use techniques or focus.

* Stmaina checks can be used to resist discomfort, or prolong actions beyond when they should normally end. Forcing yourself through something through sheer force of will is an example of a stamina check.

Endurance: A measure of a character's physical Toughness, and how difficult they are to injure. Determines a character's natural DT, as well as their pain tolerance.

Constitution: A measure of the efficiency with which a character's body operates, and how difficult it is to disturb those operations. Constitution determines a character's natural damage reduction, their DP, their temperature tolerance, and their toxic resistance.

A character with high constitution will also have a much easier time keeping themselves composed in stressful circumstances, such as being startled.

* Constitution is used to passively resist involuntary reactions, such as being startled, being nauseated, or paralyzed.

Mind: A category of traits that, together, offer an idea of a character's mental prowess. These traits represent a character's ability to think and problem solve, these tend to be very important for investigative characters.
Mind includes Intelligence, Knowledge, and Wisdom.

Mind and its subordinate traits are considered to be Insights.

Intelligence: A measure of the efficiency with which a character's mind operates, its clarity and quickness. Intelligence determines how many abilities can be held in memory, how difficult it is to retrieve forgotten abilities, and how well a character can process information. Intelligence also determines one's ability to perform mental functions, such as arithmetic.

* Intelligence Checks can be used to double the experience gained from previous checks.

* During sleep abilities can be swapped in and out of memory freely. This effect ends upon waking up.

Wisdom: A measure of a character's ability to think in abstract terms, grasp deeper concepts, and their creativity. Wisdom determines the maximum potency of cast spells, how difficult it is to modify one's spells, as well as a character's aptitude for logical reasoning, and critical thinking. Art appreciation, riddles, and philosophy are all within the realm of Wisdom.

Knowledge: A measure of the vastness of generic information kept in a character's mind at all times. This includes trivia, world information, facts, history, all manner of things one might stumble across in life.
Most often this trait is used to gain extra context surrounding specific situations, or advantages on potentially difficult checks. One may think of this trait as hints or assistance. As it determines nothing beyond a character's access to relevant information that can be used to empower their other abilities.

Weight: The heaviness of an object or entity. Weight is context sensitive. The weight value of entities is a different measure than the weight of items.
In reference to entities;
Weight 1: Small human.
Weight 2: Underweight human.
Weight 3: Average weight human.
Weight 4: Overweight Human.
Weight 5: Massive Human.

* Elves, having hollow bones, are naturally lighter than humans (-1) of an equivalent build, while ogres are naturally heavier (+1).

Entity Weight is not based on body fat. It includes things like height and muscle mass as well. It's based to think of weight as how big a character is, whether that's height, width, or circumference.

Item weight refers to the weight of weapons and items, generally this graded on the usual 1-5 scale, but it can go higher when neccesary.
Weight 1: A baseball.
Weight 2: A hardcover Book.
Weight 3: A carpenter's hammer.
Weight 4: A car battery.
Weight 5: A manhole cover.

* Carry weight: Character's have two weight limits when it comes to holding items in their inventory. Pocket limits, and the character's carry limit.
When pockets are overweight, they are at risk of ripping.
When a character's carry limit is overweight, they gain +1 to their weight class, and -1 to their athleticism. This effect is amplified the more overweight the character is.
In extreme circumstances, endurance checks must be performed to avoid knee & back injuries.

Luck: Luck is an objective, quantifiable measure of how much God loves you.

Abilities: Traits, skills, insights, classes, and techniques are all abilities. All checks are ability checks. Abilities are any thing that your character can do. Infact, it wouldn't be wrong to say abilities are the core of a character, not traits, but of one's abilities, traits are the most fundnemental.

Skills: Abilities that require a certain physical fitness, coordination, body awareness, or that raise one's heartrate when performed. These are physical talents such as martial arts or dance.
Skills require SKL Cards to focus their checks, or to perform their class techniques.

Insights: Abilities that require mental fortitude, a high level of education, or creative thinking. These are things a character is educated in, and has a great understanding of.
Insights require INS Cards to focus their checks or to perform their class techniques.

Classes: Specialized knowledge in a subject. Classes do not restrict a character, and they can have as many as they can find time to study. Technique Classes grat special Techniques for every rank, as well as class bonuses. Technique classes exist only for mechanical purposes for things such as combat and item & spell creation. For all other purposes access to class checks are the benefit, as class checks are always significantly more specific and tailored to certain tasks often producing much more favorable results.

Techniques: techniques are special abilities that offer mechanical benefits to the character in contexts such as combat, item & spell creation, or that modify one's checks. They cost cards of relavant type to perform.

Memory: Characters may only access abilities that they have in memory. On the Character Sheet, memory is indicated by clear, white slots, while forgotten slots are darker, and blurred.
Forgotten abilities will have their experience decay faster than one's in memory at midnight of every night. Abilities can freely be forgotten, but may only be moved back into memory once per hour upon a successful intelligence check.

Memory is determined by one's Intelligence.

Bonuses: Modifiers applied to one's traits, raising or lowering them. Bonuses come in two forms, Trait Bonuses affect only a single trait, while Category bonuses affect all traits within a category, such as Mind, Body, Athleticism, or Awareness.

Experience: Experience in an ability determines at what point a character gains a new Rank, thus experience is only earned for classes and traits.
Successful ability checks award one point of experience, critical successes award two points, and passing checks with penalties award an aditional point for every penalty.
Checks will earn one less experience point for every advantage used.

* This means the maximum amount of experience that can be earned from one check is 5 from a check for a rank 4 class, with three penalties, no advantages, and a pair of ones (1, 1).
This can then be multiplied by an intelligence check for a maximum total of 10 experience.

Satiety: A measure of a character's basic needs. These needs are food, water, and sleep. satiety points, or "Sat" are spent to refill one's cards. this can be done at any time outside of combat.
It costs 1 sat to refill 1 card.

Food sat is a measure of calories consumed, with 1 sat being the equivalent to approximately 100 calories.
Water sat is a measure of water consumed (Not liquid) with 1 sat being equivalent to 8 fluid ounces, or one cup.
Sleep sat is a measure of the hours one can remain comfortably awake, with 1 sat being equivalent to 1 hour, and every hour of sleep granting 2 sat.

* Actions can be performed to refill sat aside from the normal means, such as slapping yourself awake to regain some sleep sat.

* During sleep abilities can be swapped in and out of memory freely. This effect ends upon waking up.

Sleep: Sleep is a neccesary resource for all mundane creatures, and is one of three forms of Satiety. When a character's sleep satiety drops below half it's maximum, they are able to fall asleep with no penalities. Attempting to sleep while awake, or under certain effects requires a stamina check which, if failed, will result in the character being unable to sleep for another hour.
When a character falls asleep, it's directing player will roll to determine how long the character will sleep comfortably. The sum of the roll can be focused using the character's con at no cost.
Sleeping characters are vulnerable. They are incabable of acting, or defending themselves, until they are awoken.

* Sleeping characters are also most in touch with the supernatural.

Toxicity: The amountby which a character is affected by intoxicants. These include things like drugs, alcohol, and poisons, as well as the positive effects of potions, special food items, or spells. Toxicity will also be used to track the duration of things like illnesses, Injuries, and special character specific effects.
-World Aspects-

Time: Every hour in RATTRAPS is played, from when a character wakes to when they sleep that night.
One hour in RATTRAPS is time enough for a character to accomplish two things. Whether that be two tasks, two complex steps in a large task, or hold a conversation that spans two topics in detail.
Every hour will drain a character's satiety, each by 1 sat, and progress the temperature.

Temperature effects will occur when the hour is passed. Meaning if a character finds shelter before they pass the hour on their character sheet, they may avoid some of the harsh effects of exposure.

Every night at midnight, when the hour is passed, experience for all abilities will decay by one. Forgotten abilities will decay based on a percentage determined by their mind tratis. Consecutive hours spent in the elements will also build to certain effects, such as one's cloths becoming saturated with sweat in the heat, or water in intense humidity.

Temperature: The current heat of the outdoors, determined by the region's climate, and daily weather conditions.
The temperature fluctuates every hour, but days will always be hottest near noon, and coldest near midnight.

Character's reaction to the temperature is determined by their constitution, but they can further protect themselves from extreem heat or cold by wearing suitable clothes. All clothes increase a character's insulation, making them warmer, thus removing clothes can make one cooler. Certain specialized garments can also make one cooler in particularly hot climates.

Always dress for the weather!

* The Context Modifier is a slider located at the top of the character sheet that strongly adjusts the temperature. this is used when a character has entered a context that overrides the natural temperature. Such contexts include sitting beside a fire, being indoors, swimming, or magical effects.

Humidity: The amount of water held in the air. Humidity when too high or low will cause special effects on those who are exposed for too many hours, such as nose bleeds, or cracked lips when it's too dry, or sweating & saturation when it's too wet.

Daily humidity is also one of two determining factors for rain, which is more common when the water content in the air is higher.

Wind: The natural movement of air. Wind has a cooling effect that is unique from that of temperatures. Wind cools the body by passsing through the clothes, and replacing the insulated air, with new cold air. Thus to protect one's self from the wind, Clothing with a high resistance to absorbance is neccessry.

* Spells that generate force also create wind as a byproduct, and thus can be used to create cooling effects.

Rain: When the day's forecast check passes both humidity and wind, it will rain for that hour. Rain is more common when both wind and humidity in the region is high. Functionally rain lasts for only one hour, but when conditions are right, multiple consecutive hours can have rain, when this occurs, the G.M. can use other attributes in the forcast to decide the strength of the storm as well as any world affects it may have such as floods or landslides (Which may also be used as tarot encounters).

Files: Over the course of a run, characters may collect written information in the form of notes, books, diaries, and letters. These are items that may need to be referenced several times over the course of a run and thus, their contents will be tracked in the "Files" section on the character's sheet.
These entries can be expanded by the G.M. as more information is gained from the same source.

Notes: During a character's journey through a run, they will be exposed to many details, characters, and places. RATTRAPS has no "Story" Or "Plot", every run follows the decisions and exploits of it's cast. For this reason it's reccomended that both directing players and G.M. keep detailed notes on relavant information and mysteries with desireable answers.
To this end, every character's sheet has a notes section.

* This section is also where quirks are to be noted for the G.M.'s reference.

Tarot: The G.M. will draw cards from a standard 4 suit, 52 card deck to deciede random encounters, special finds, and the day to day happenings in the world.
The cards will decide the event based on the Tarot System, in which each suit represents a style of encounter, and each number represent it's magnitude, significance, or specifically what it is.
-Combat-

Weapons: A weapon is any item held with offensive intent. These items are used to amplify a character's offensive power, and focus them into stronger attacks.

Weapons come primarily in two forms;
Melee: Swords, axes, spears, etc. that attack to a specic consistent range, and are powered by the acting character's ability.

Ranged: Bows, slings, firearms, etc. that have no hard limit to the distance at which they can land attacks, and are powered by mechanisms contained within the weapon.

All weapons concentrate a character's abilities through their "weapon stats". These include;
- Sharpness
- Range
- Balance
- Shape

Attacks: Offensive actions with the intent to cause harm to another entity.
When performing an attack, characters will need to perform two rolls, first Target, and then To-Hit.

The Target roll is not a check. It simply determines what locations on the target are most available to be struck. In the case of mundane creatures, one dice is used to represent the limb while the other represents the spot on that limb. Which is which is up to the discretion of the directing player. Character's can use focus to target less armored, or more vulnerable spots on their opponent.

Then the acting character will perform their To-Hit roll. This roll determines if and how hard the character will land their attack.
This check is performed using a relavant offensive ability class or Dexterity with a penalty.

- If this check passes, the attack will land at the intended power.
- If the check passes with a pair, the damage dealt will be doubled.

If the check fails, melee weapons will deal half damage, while ranged weapons will miss to a degree equal to how badly the check failed.

If the check fails with a pair, melee weapons will miss entirely, and leave the acting character destabilized for the moment, while ranged attacks will miss severely, striking the worst available mark.

Grappling: The act of one character physically restraining or wrestling another entity into submission.
When two entities enter a grapple they most both roll, the sum of both dice, their strength, and their weight, determines their grapple advantage.
This means heavier, stronger characters will be more difficult to win grapples against.

* Having certain ability classes can be used as an additional grapple advantage, but these advantages will alway be worth 1 regarcless of rank.
Only Grappling Classes may add their full rank to a grapple check.

Attack Power: The intended force put into an attack. This value ranges from 1-5, and modifies the damage and speed of an attack.
Level 1: No recovery, half damage.
Level 2: 1 recovery, reduced damage.
Level 3: 2 recovery, full damage.
Level 4: 3 recovery, increased damage.
Level 5: 4 recovery, double damage.

Recovery moments are moments in a round when the acting character is commited to the attack, and cannot perform any actions. These are les severe on characters with high speed, as their actions take less time than those of slower characters.
The structure of an attack, where recovery & hit moments fall is decided by the directing player, but recovery moments must me adjacent to their hit moment.

* Power Levels only apply to melee attacks. Ranged weapons all have their own damage modifiers.

Bows can be drawn to a power level equal to or below their draw weight.

The power level of firearms is determined by the powder used in it's ammunition, as well as how much is loaded.


Hit Moments: The moment in a round when a melee attack lands. This moment must be surrounded by any necessary recovery moments. If the hit moments of two attacking characters overlap, the attacks will clash, with the stronger attack parrying the weaker one, destabilizing that attacker. relative to how much stronger the winning attack was.

* Certain techniques can be performed out of these clashes to avoid the risk of being parried.

* A character's resistance to being parried is determined by their Speed.

* Even when parried or destabilized, characters can escape treacherous situations using the free technique scramble.
* The hit moment of ranged attacks is determined by the distance to the target. Attacks within the flat effective range of a ranged weapon will land the moment they are fired. for every multiple of a weapon's flat effective range away the hit-moment will advance one moment later.

For example: If a bow's effective range is 10 spaces, and the target is 30 spaces away when the attack is fired. the hit moment is two moments later.

* Projectile use speed 5 moments, regardless of the acting character's abilities.


Damage Threshold: The maximum damage that can be absorbed by something before it breaks. The amount of damage that is reduced by clothing & armor is a portion of it's DT. When recieving damage, if the damage dealt to a piece of armor is greater than it's DT, the armor is destroyed at that spot. It's DT will be subtracted from the damage value and, and the new damage value will be dealt to the layers beneath.

* only when armor is broken will it lend it's full DT to stopping the attack that broke it. This is an armor piece's desperate attempt to defend it's wearer, and the wearer's final warning before things get dangerous.
All items have a DT, because all things can be broken given enough force. DT is determined by the material & construction of an item. It is immutable unless through magic, or mechanical reinforcement.

Padding: The portion of an armor piece's DT that will always reduce incoming damage. This is determined by the amor's construction. A crude metal plate is difficult to break, but easily transmits force through it, while padded jackets are easy to pierce, but absorb force well.
Due to this, proper armor should be layered, with padding protected under high a high DT outerlayer.

Deflection: Certain types of armor encourage incoming attacks to deflect. Most often this is due to it's curved, or grooved shapes. An armor's deflection rating is added as penalties to the incoming attack's To-Hit roll. The attack will also gain an additional penlalty for every rank of sharpness the weapon has above 3.
Blunt weapons nullify deflection based on how far from 3 Sharpness they are. Meaning a weapon with sharpness 1 can ignore two points of DT.

Balance: How a melee weapon holds it's weight. Specifically, "balance" refers to the amount of weight a weapon holds at it's base, while "Weight" in this context is the weight held at the end of it's effective range.
A higher "weight" means higher damage swinging attacks, as well as more penalties when trying to focus thrusting attacks.
Generally you want a swinging weapon with a high "weight", and thrusting weapons to have high balance.

Sharpness: The degree to which force will be focused & amplified. Sharpness comes in two forms, Edge and Point. Edge sharpness is used for swinging attacks, while point is used for thrusting attacks. The benefit of sharpness is amplified by a character's dexterity, having both high will result in incredibly damaging attacks. However high sharpness also increases the chance for deflection, as every point of sharpness above three adds a penalty to the To-Hit roll of attacks targeting armor with the Deflection property, while blunt weapons will nullify deflection by adding advantages relative to how low their sharpness is.
Sharpness is lowered when a weapon is chipped too many times, and raised when sharpened by an entity.

Chips: Whenever a weapon absorbs damage equal to half it's DT, it will recieve a chip. When the number of chips is equal to the weapon's Retention the weapon's sharpness is lowered.
The chips for edge and point sharpness are tracked seperately, thus it's possible to dull the edge of a sword, while it's point remains razor sharp.
Chips may be removed through regular maintenence of a weapon.

* Chips may also be added when a weapon is mistreated in someway.

Retention: The ability of a material to hold it's shape, and resist chipping. Retention determines the number of chips a weapon can recieve before it's relavant sharpness is dulled. Retention like DT is a property of the material the weapon is made of, and thus cannot be modified except through magic or mechanical reinforcement.

Length: The literal length of a weapon, it's maximum reach. Length is always the range used for thrusting attacks, unless modified by special techniques.
Length however, is seperate from effective range, and it's possible for an attack to deal reduced damage for landing at the end of it's length, beyond it's effective range.

* Certain weapons may use their length as the effective range for thrusting attacks, while the range value is used for swinging attacks.

Range: A melee weapon's effective range determines whether or not it will deal it's full damage or not. attacks that land on targets too close, or too far will have their damage reduced relative to how far away from the "sweet spot" the target is when the attack lands. For this reason it may be preferable to rush in on opponent's equipped with long weapons in order to reduce the incoming damage as much as possible.

A Ranged Weapon doesn't have a "sweet spot" and will always deal it's intended damage to the target, unless deflected. All ranged weapons have ranges that determine the distance a target can be before incurring a target penalty. Ranged weapons can attack to any distance beyond their effective range, but will incur more penalties the further the target is.

* The effective range of bot melee and ranged weapons can be modified by certain techniques.

Shape Modifier: Certain weapons are designed to impart a mechanical advantage to swinging or thrusting attacks. These amplify the damage dealt my the relavant attack type.
Shape modifiers are based on the structure of a weapon, and cannot be modified without changing the weapon's shape through magic of mechanical means.

Items: Any object that can be carried. Items include tools, weapons, armor & clothing articles, and any object one might squirrel away in their pockets.
Items are kept in a character's inventory and can serve nearly any purpose.
The two most common use of items, is as aid in combat, in the form of bombs, traps, buffs, or debuffs, and for context advantages, such as notes, books, tools, etc.
In combat
-Combat-
Combat: The use of violence to achieve moral or strategic goals. Combat begins when any entity submits a Battle Ticket to the G.M.. This character will get the first round of combat all to themselves. After this first round, all involved entities will denote their actions on their battle tickets, and once all neccesary tickets are submitted to the G.M., all actions will occur simultaneously.
Combat lasts until the directing player requests it to end, however this means that other entites may turn in another ticket to gain a free first round to act. So exercise caution.
It's best to only end combat when one is sure all their enemies are dead, restrained, routed, or surrendering.

Battle-Ticket: A list of actions to be taken in the current round of combat. The battle ticket must be submitted to the G.M. before the results of the round can be narrated. The only exception to this rule being when a character spends an insight card to submit a partial ticket.
When submitting a partial ticket, characters may submit actions only up to certain breakpoints denoted by the alternating colors of the actions on one's character's sheet.
This allows a directing player to see what happens in the set moments before commiting to the rest of their ticket.

Round: One "turn" of combat, or one set of battle tickets. Every round is seperated into moments, upon which actions may be taken. At the end of every round damage will be sustained from any lesser bleed stacks on involved entities. If the damage sustained causes any entity's HP to reach zero, they will be downed.

* Every round lasts roughly 10 seconds.

Moments: Subdivisions of a round based on the acting character's speed. On any moment an action can be performed. When all tickets are submitted to the G.M., all roundes occur simultaneously. Attacks, and the consquences of actions can interrupt or cancel the actions of other characters, based on when they occur. For this reason, it's important to ensure one has the space and time necessary to perform their intended actions without being intterupted.

Actions: Any act performed is one action. For example;
- Pulling your backpack off.
- Opening the backpack.
- Finding an item inside.
- Closing the backpack.
- putting your back back on.
- Using the item.

The above process consists of six actions. Meaning it would take any un-altered human at least two rounds to use an item kept in such a back pack.
Actions also include movement, attacks, communication, and defense.
Without the use of special techniques, the only action that can be performed along with another is movement, and even then it can only be combined with non-offensice, and non-defensive actions.

* Certain inventory pockets allow Quick-Access to their contained items, this means the items can be produced in a single action, without needing to open or locate them.

Quick-Access: Certain articles that provide pockets grant the Quick-Access technique to characters attempting to retrieve contained items. In combat, this means that the pocket's contents can be freely accessed and produced in a single action.
Such articles include utility belts, bandoliers, holsters, and scabbards.

Destabilization: The process of being momentarily knocked off-balance, and unable to act.
Destabilization occurs after and attack is parried, after landing from a jump, or when stuck by certain offensive techniques.
inflicting, and capitalizing on destablization through the use of tactics & techniques is a core component of combat in RATTRAPS.

Evasive Movement: Quick emergency movement, that most often costs a skill card to perform and is the only action that can be performed while destabilized. All characters have access to evasive movement in the form of the Scramble technique.
Evasive movement has no effect on incoming damage, and evading characters can still be hit while evading. For this reason it's best to carefully choose the direction one evades carefully, as closing the distance on an enemy using a long weapon may be more advantageous than avoiding the hit all together.

Health Points: An abstract measure of an entity's overall health. HP is determined for, mundane entities, entirely by size. HP determines how quickly entities succumb to wounds, and how quickly they bleed out.
Characters may lose HP due to illness, injuries, or satiety deprivation, but most often HP will be lost during combat through bleeding.
Hp is regained naturally at a rate of 1 HP per hour. This effect stops when any form of satiety is zero, and natural healing can be amplified through the use of spells and medicine.
When a character's HP reaches zero that character is considered down.

Bleeding: Blood loss can be caused in a variety of ways, and is the primary form of dealing damage to characters in combat.
There are two kinds of bleeds characters can sustain;

Lesser Bleeds: Caused by bruising, or minor cuts. These are caused by any damage sustained by a character that does not pass that character's bleed threshold. The damage taken is placed in an empty bleed stack, and the character will take 1 damage at the start of every round, until they've taken the full damage value they sustained.
Character's may have five active lesser bleed stacks at a time. Meaning the max amount of damage a character can sustain from lesser bleeds is 5 hp per round, thus, a character with full bleed stacks may only remain active for a maximum of five rounds before they are downed.
Lesser bleeds are a slow killer, once damage starts piling on a character it's only a matter of time before they can no longer continue fighting.
Once combat is over, bleed stacks are totalled, halved, and that value is then subtracted from the character's HP. If the character's HP is reduced to zero, they are rendered unconcious, a state identical to sleep.
Lesser bleeds END when a character is downed. bleed stacks CAN NOT passively consume DP.

Greater Bleeds:When a character sustains significant damage, such that the value dealt surpasses their bleed threshold. A Greater Bleed is caused. The greater bleed will last one round for every ten points of damage above the bleed threshold the character sustained, with a minimum of one round.
During every round of the greater bleed, the affected character will lose 10 HP at a rate equal to 2HP every speed 5 moment. This will be tracked by the G.M., and progresses as the affected character submits their tickets.
Greater bleeds can only be stopped by characters a relavant ability. once staunched, either through medical intervention, or naturally, fatal bleeds will become a permanant lesser bleed stack.
If a character goes down, with an active greater bleed that greater bleed will deplete the character's DP by 1 every round, for as many rounds as is left in the greater bleed.

* When a character's HP reaches 0, they can perform a stamina check to force themselves concious. If this is successful, HP remains zero, they may continue to act, but the check must be repeated every round with an additional penalty, and any hit will knock them straight into unconciousness, with no chance to resist.

* Bleed threshold is determined by a character's constitution.

Injuries: When a character sustains damage that exceeds their EDT, they must roll an endurance check that will determine if they are injured. If the check fails with a pair, the character's limb will be crippled, doubling both the negative effects, and recovery time of the injury.
Injured limbs will grant penalties to any check that requires their use as well as the debuffs listed on the Armor Grids. While crippled limbs cannot be used until they heal, and once their recovery time is past, they become injured for the same time they were crippled.

* Crippled and injured limbs, have a chance to impart negative quirks on characters.

Armor-Grids: When attacking characters, attacks will land on specific spots on the target's body. this is tracked using the Armor Grid system. The armor grid tracks the coverage of protective clothing. attacks will need to transfer force through all layers of armor to deal damage to the character.
Damage is past through the grids from right to left, meaning the armor displayed furthest right will be the first to break when attacked.
A proper armor setup should have high DT articles covering high PD articles beneath. This way the character gains the defense from the bottom articles, while the top layer protects them from breaking.

Repelled Damage: When dealing damage with an attack, all damage not absorbed by the target, is repelled, and dealt back into the object used to attack. This can cause chipping, and sometimes even break weapons on successful strong hits.

* When attacking unarmed, repelled damage is dealt into the attacking character's own limbs.

Limbs: Humanoid characters have six "limbs"; Head, two arms, two legs, and the torso. These limbs are covered by armor pieces. Certain types of armor pieces may cover multiple limbs. These limbs are then targeted specifically by attacks.
All human limbs have five targetable spots, smaller targets will haver fewer, larger targets may have more.
Limbs are numbered from left to right, top to bottom on the Armor Grid. They are as follows;
1. Left Arm
2. Head
3. Right Arm
4. Left Leg
5. Body
6. Right Leg

Armor coverage is displayed on the grids with check boxes, when an armor piece's DT is exceeded by the damage done, it's broken. that spot is unchecked, and damage can then pass through to the next layer.
When armor breaks it's DT is subtracted from the damage dealt by the attack before the damage is passed through to the next layer.

Spots: The subdivisions of limbs that are individually targetable by incoming attacks. All human limbs have five targetable spots, while smaller creatures may have fewer. Attacks targeting spots that a limb doesn't have will miss entirely.

* Spot 6 on the arms targets held items, like weapons, and can be used to disarm enemies, or even shatter their weapons!

Death-Points: An entity's final layer of defense against death! When a character is downed, any subsequent attack reduces DP by 1. The only exception to this rule is for characters with access to the Killing Blow technique, which is an attack that may instantly kill targets if it's damage reaches their DP.
Characters that are downed during active greater bleeds will lose 1 DP per round, for as long as the greater bleed lasts.

* Character's at low HP with greater bleeds are in critical condition, and should be attended to immediately to prevent their death!

Going Down: When a character's HP reaches zero for any reason, they are considered "Downed". They collapse, and must perform stamina checks to remain concious. All actions require relavant checks to perform. and all checks have additional penalties. Downed characters are at their Most vulnerable!
If a character goes down outside of combat, they lost conciousness. This state is identical to sleep.
-Utility-
Maps: Maps will be provided by the G.M. upon characters entering key locations, upon request from a directing player, or when combat is initiated.
Maps are divided into spaces, each space is roughly three feet, or one meter on each side.
Humans are considered two spaces tall

Spaces: The standard unit of measurement in RATTRAPS. spaces are roughly 3 feet, or 1 meter on each side.
In practice it's better to think of these spaces as cubes. Humans areconsidered to be two spaces tall. This is the amount of space that's comfortable to occupy. passing through tighter spaces will depend on a character's weight.

Rough Terrain: Any space that is difficult to move through. This could mean mud, thick vegetation, or deep snow.
When moving through rough terrain a character's movement speed is halved. Meaning it'll take two actions move cardinally, and four actions to pass diagonally through these spaces.
Additionally, destabilizing effects when on rough terrain are worsened, either increasing the number of moments, a character is off balance, or knocking them prone.
When a character has moved consecutively through as many rough terrain spaces as they have ranks in stamina, they will lose one skill card. If they have no skill cards left, they will roll an endurance check to sustain a minor injury.

Outside of combat, the same rules still apply, but character's can move more slowly, reducing their risk of injury.

* Terrain can be altered or negated using tools, magic, or items. vegetation can be cut or burned away, snow can be melted or walked over easily with snow shoes.
Always be prepared to deal with the environment!

Movement: During combat, characters may move one space cardinally (Up, down, left, or right) per action, and half a space diagonally.
Half space movements are only considered when moving consecutively. If a character moves diagonally, performs an action without movement, and then moves again, they are free to move in any direction.
half steps are only counted when passing through spaces.
movement can occur simultaneously with any action that is niether offensive, nor defensive, unless through the use of special techniques. Most often this includes, reloading a weapon, preparing an item, speaking, accessing one's inventory, or operating mechanisms.

Dashing: Characters may spend a skill card to double their movement speed. This effect lasts until their consecutive movement ends. While dashing, no non-movement actions can be performed without the use of certain special techniques.
Dashing characters must still count half steps when passing diagonally through spaces.

* Because dashing only ends when the acting character's consecutive movement is broken, this means that if a character has access to a technique that allows actions to be taken during a dash, other techniques such as Fletch, evasive movements, or Harvest Flurry can be taken without breaking the dash, thus doubling the range of some offensive techniques.

Climbing: If a character's strength is equal to their weight, they can climb easy surfaces with no restrictions. If it's higher they gain advantages when scaling more difficult surfaces.
Scaling any surface difficult enough to require a check will be considered moving through rough terrain.
Vertical movement through climbing is treated the same as normal movement, one space per action, 2 when dashing.

Jumping: Characters can jump to a vertical height of their speed -1. For every space laterally they move, one space of height is lost. characters move through the air 2 spaces per moment, and are considered destabilized while off the ground. Jumping characters will be destabilized as well the moment after they land.

* Jumping during a dash will double the lateral distance a character can cover during their jump without increasing the loss to vertical height.

* Character's can use evasive movement to exit their landing destabilization.

* If a character is struck, or acted upon in the air, a speed check must be performed when landing to avoid falling, and becoming prone.

Prone: The state of being off one's feet. Prone character's have their movement speed, and attack damage halved. Prone characters cannot use skill techniques unless specifically mentioned otherwise.
Prone characters must spend an action to get back to their feet.

Inventory: The stock of items kept on a character. Character's Inventory are represented by the pockets of their current Tack. A character's inventory is limited to what they can hold in both arms, what fits in their pockets, and what they can have equipped.
When the content's of a character's inventory exceeds their carry weight, the character's weight will increase relative to how overweight their inventory is. Encumbered characters will have their athleticism reduced by one.

Pockets: Any bag, pouch, or holster currently in a character's Tack will add itself to the character's inventory. All pockets have a weight limit determined by the quality of their construction, and the sturdiness of the material they're made from. Pockets also contain slots that may house items who's size does not exceed the number of slots present in the pocket.

Size: Size in reference to items measures how many slots they take up within a character's inventory.
Items with size may NOT overlap. However Items with zero size may occupy the space left in large items.
Size zero items take up only as much space as their name, and thus can be abreviated to save space. However, if a directing player can ever not remember what an item is, that item is considered lost, and may turn up again in the world, or be found by other characters.

Tack: All eqquiped items, including jewlery, clothes, armor, and weapons. Tacked items do not add to a character's weight limit, and most tacked items will contribute to a character's resistances.
Weapons must be tacked before they can be used, and characters cannot have more weapons tacked than they can hold in both hands. Unless those weapons have scabbards, sheaths, or holsters, at which point, they no longer require the use of a character's arms to be readied.

Resistances: A character's abiility to endure the elements. Most commonly this refers to a character's Insulation, Absorbance, Conductivity, and Brilliance. Which are determined by the articles one wears.
Insulation: A character's resistance to changes in temperature, and how well their tacked articles contain heat.
Insulation also determines how much heat is needed to ignite a material, and set it on fire. As well as how quickly that fire will spread.

Absorbance: A character's resistance to being saturated by liquids, and thus inhereiting their special effects. Absorbance also determines how long an article will take to dry when it's saturated (6-Abs hours).

Conductivity: A character's resistance to electric currents. Electricity will always target the object or entity within it's range with the highest conductivity.

Brilliance: How striking a character is to the senses. This affects how a character is percieved socially, as well as how easy or difficult they are to target with magic.

* When saturated with dirt, a character's brilliance becomes zero, regardless of what they're wearing.

Articles: Anything that is "worn" by a character, including jewelery, clothes, shoes, and armor. All articles contribute to a character's resistances, but not necesarily to their Armor Grid.

Storage: When character's set down or discard items from their inventory that they may want to reaquire later, they may place them in one of the three storage sections on their sheets. These sections function identically to the character's inventory, and serve only to help track what items were left where. They can also be named by the directing players to better help track where these items are located.

* Certain techniques such as maintaining a Summoner's Vault require the use of a storage section to represent the vault's contents. Other permanent uses for storage sections include wagons, and pack animals.

Retirement: At any moment a character may retire from the run. This may occur for a variety of reasons. The character's goals may take them away from the current events & locations of the current run into their own spin-off, They may leave when things become too dangerous, or Directing players may become unable to participate in the run, and choose to retire their characters.

Death: All things that are born must one day die. Death is unavoidable. All a person has is their time before departure. It's imperative that that time is well spent. One cannot wait for joy to come to them, as no garuntee exists that they will survive to see it come.
If one cannot be content with life in the now, then life is being wasted. Through understanding, patience, and a greater awareness no life need be left in vain.
We all must leave one day on our own, thus our relationship with ourselves must be a loving one. We are all that we have in the end.

When a character's DP reach zero, for any reason, they die.

Entities: Any thing that can act and will is an entity. Entities are distinct only from objects, which are by nature, inanimate.
There exist two kinds of entities, mundane, and ethereal. Mundane entities being people & animals, and ethereal entities being supernatural creatures like ghosts & demons.

* It is possible through special means for an entity to transcend it's nature, and change from mundane to ethereal, or the reverse.

Objects: Any inanimate object, including weapons, items, rocks, furniture. These are distinct from entities which have their own will, or ability to act on the things around them.

Mundanity: To be native to in the natural world. Mundanity is the realm of science, and physicality. Animals, people, plants, rocks, furniture, and weapons nearly all exist as mundane items. Things that obey the natural laws, and are physically tangible. Mundane life operates as biologocal machines, consuming necessary fuels, that are then circulated via tubes through the use of pumps and pressurized systems. The mundane world is one of science, and engineering. Mundane objects and entities have no normal means of interacting with ethereal entities or objects, though they are known to have some dominion over the ethereal elements.

Ethereality: To be entirely foreign in nature to the natural world. Ethereality is the realm of the fluid & abstract. It is a world where force of thought reign over physical violence. One entirely alien to what mundane creatures can understand. Ethereal objects and entities have no normal means of interacting with the mundane, but are known to have some dominion over the natural elements.

* This is the source of all magic.

Magic: The ability of a mundane entity to use the dominion of ethereal entites over the natural elements, to bend the natural laws in their favor.
All magic is some form of communication between ethereal and mundane entities to affect the natural state of things.

More practically speaking, all magic must be cast through an entity of the opposing nature. Which is to say; All magic is the commanding of ethereal entities to exercise their influence over the natural elements.
This means that for a spell to be cast, a character must have some sort of connection to the unnatural, as well as an ability to communicate their intention (Spell ).
Spells exist in-game as Insight Techniques.
* With certain exceptions.

These techniques when used function similiarly to attacks, in that they require two rolls.
The Connection roll, which functions similiarly to an attack's target roll, and the Cast check, which functions similiarly to an attack's To-Hit roll.

Connection: To target an entity or object with magic, a character must be able to focus on them either by physically touching it, visually seeing it, or mentally imagining it. These methods all take the form of different rolls with the exception of physical touch. Making physical contact with the intended target has no chance of failure.
When connection visually, characters must pass a perception check. While connecting mentally, they must pass an intelligence check.
Connection checks are all subject to the usual penalties one might encounter. Failing this check will risk the spell targeting something unintended. perception based connection checks function identically when missing to ranged weapon misses, while intelligence based connection checks risk targeting items of similiar description.
For example a failed intelligence connection check meant to target a blonde enemy, might strike a different blonde character in the vicinity.

Creating connections is easier on targets with high brilliance, and lower on targets with low brilliance. For this reason dressing plainly is advantageous when facing magic users.

* Characters can make as many connections of one type as they have ranks in the relavant trait, however every current connection is added as a penalty to additional connection rolls.

* Spells must assert how many connections they can accept in their definition.

Casting: A spell's "To-Hit" roll, this determines if your spell goes off, as well as how effective it is.
All Casting rolls are ability checks, with the relavant ability in question being any understanding of an ethereal entity.

Failing a cast check means the entity will not fully understand your intent, this could lead to a variety of different failures dependant on the spell you're using. Failing with a pair risks angering the entreated entity, adding penalties to future casting attempts, while passing with a pair may amplify the effects of the spell, or potentially provide other benefits based on context or the entity.

* Not all entites have influence over all elements, and thus some entities will be able to offer more powerful results in certain dominions than others.

Elements: The natural elements are what create the mundane world, the manipulation of these forces are refered to based on their methods; They can be exploited through mechanical principles through science and engineering, or manipulated through cooperation with the ethereal through magic.
All elements define existence itself, and no one is superior to another. They are equivalent peers extant in harmony with one another.
The natural Elements are listed below;

Permeability: How readily one accepts another. The strength of boundaries between substances. Without permeability all the world would collapse into a great dissolution, much like the natural state of ethereality. Permeability is expressed in the very existence of distinction, individuality exists because one insists upon itself.
A more digestible example is a material's resistance to absorbance. Articles weak to absorbance readily accept the properties of fluids poured onto them, some even become permanantly stained, never to return to how they once were. A change due only to contact with another substance.
Another example would be a character's constitution, their resistance to the alterative effects of medicine & toxins.

* DT & PD are also attributes within the dominion of permeability, as they determine how a material reacts and accepts forces.

Heat: The existence of motion in stillness. Heat the opposite of heat is not cold, but heat's absence, as the opposite of motion is stillness.
An obvious experession of this element is in the day's temperature, and the ability of insulated clothing to resist it's change.
Though, a more accurate representation would be satiety, as mundane creatures must consume fuel, which is then burned internally to generate their movements, this energy is expended more quickly through actions requiring the use of cards.

This sudden, and at times violent, transfer of energy can, at times create intense heat as well as repulsive or attractive forces.
The opposite of electricity is an ethereal phenomenon known as masaic charges. this ethereal force behaves identical to electricity but only bears it's effect in the ethereal world.

Time: The continuous passage from one state to the next. Time is what allows the world to progress. Without the passage of time life would have no meaning.

Space: The dimensions of height, width, and depth. The element that allows the existence of distance.
Space is most obviously expressed in the existence of spaces on combat fields, or the measure of one's height & movement speed.
The opposite of space is anti-space. Pockets of unnatural space that exist in tandem with natural spaces.

Force: The physical pressure created in the collision of matter. Kinetic forces are most easily observed in the existence of damage, which is simply the mechanical generation of force, amplified through sharpness and shape modifiers.
However the most accurate expression of elemental force is gravity, the attraction of objects to their greaters.

Substance: Mass, and the material properties. Substance defines what a thing is, and how it reacts to it's peers & pressures.

Spells: There exist sixteen basic spells, which may be combined by character's that know them into more complex, and specialized compound spells. Compound spells are the most common, and useful spells a character can know.
Spells are all considered insights.
* Unless learned through certain mutations.


All basic spells have a cost of 1 • their potency. Characters may cast spells at potencies equal to or less than their wisdom, while ethereal entities may only perform spells at potencies that are equal to or less than their dominion over the relevant element.

Thus, when casting spells, the power of the spell is limited first by the acting character's wisdom, and then by the relavent entity's dominion.

Among the basic spells, there exist two kinds; Levidromic spells, who's functions invert when cast backwards, and palindromic spells, which have the same function regardless of how they're cast.
Levidromic spells are learned in only one direction at a time. Inverted spells must be learned seperately.

Listed here are all basic spells;


~Levidromic~ Generate: Create from nothing an amount determined by potency of an element at the point of connection.
When cast in reverse, Generate becomes Remove, and instead destroys an element.

* The spell Remove(Heat) is how one would "generate cold"

Seek: Detect a specific attribute or element.
When cast in reverse, Seek becomes Avoid, and can be used to direct a spell away from it's target.

* This avoidance behavior can be used to have a spell autonomously avoid detection, or detterents.

Trigger: When used in a compound spel this spell will only proceed to the following effects if it detects it's targeted attribute or element. Useful for enchantments or traps.
When cast in reverse it activates when it detects the absense of it's target. Be careful as this could mean the trigger goes off imediately upon casting, if it were cast into an enviornment where it's target doesn't exist.

* Trigger can be used with Seek, and will go off as soon as Seek finds it's target.
Potency detects the sensitivity of the trigger, with 1 requiring a great change to activate, and 5 activatinge at the faintest hint.

* The Define spell can be used to further specify what exactly sets off this spell.

Correlate: tie two connected attributes together, mirroring effects from one to the other. when cast in reverse, the relationship becomes inverted, thus when one value raises, the other lowers.
Potency has no effectm on this spell.

Amplify:Raise a connected value. When cast in reverse, amplify becomes dampen, and values can be lowered.
Potency determines how great the change is to the target.

* Amplify can be used on the potencies of other spells, allowing mundane castors to conjure frightingly powerful effects. (Up to the limit of their entity's dominion.)

Designate: Specify a connected object, designate a region on the surface of the connection, or a three-dimensional field around the point of connection for following effects to take place within.
The potency of this spell determines the maximum number of connected objects to designate, or the size of the area to act upon.
when cast in reverse, Designate becomes Exclude, and instead defines connected objects and areas to not affect with the following spells.

* In the event that this spell is used for an area effect by excluding the area the effect doesn't happen in, the distance of the effect will be determined by the relavant entity's dominion.

* Designate can be used with Seek and Connect to define a search area around the initial point of connection. The connect spell could then be used to autonomously find new targets to act on with following spells.

Breach: Open a tear in the curtain that seperates the mundane from the ethereal.
The potency of this spell determines the size of the breach.
When cast in reverse, this spell will instead close tears to the otherside.

* Because inverted spells are learned seperately, one must be very careful with this spell, so as not to open breaches they cannot close. Afterall, there's no telling what may come through something like that...

Remember: Identify the current state of the connected object.
Potency determines the accuracy of the memory.
When cast in reverse, this spell instead clears remembered states. In this case, Potency will determine how much of the memory to clear.
This spell does nothing on it's own, and must be paired with Recall or other spells to get some kind of effect.

Recall: Return to a remembered state. This spell has no function if not fed a state through either Remember or generated information.
Potency determines how accurate to the memory the recalled state is.
When cast in reverse, Recall becomes Resist, and remembered states are instead resisted.

Seal:Create a magical lock, binding ethereal entities to objects.
Potency determines the strength of the lock, and how difficult it is for the entity to escape it.
When cast in reverse Seal becomes Release, and instead breaks already placed locks. In this case, potency must be exactly equal to the potency of the lock. The use of improper release spells could be rather... Explosive in nature.

Define:Specify shape, form, or color. Define allows spells to be specific in their function. It turns shapeless repellant forces into targeted, aimable, attacks.
Define is the most important spell for the creation of compound spells.
Potency determines the specificity of the definition.
When cast in reverse Define becomes Obscure, and destroys the target organized structures, reducing them to noise.

Set:A terminal spell used to place enchantments and curses. Set spells no longer affect the caster's cards, even when the set spell includes Sustains. Potency determines how resilient the enchantment is to being desecrated.

* This spell can be learned through the Enchanter class.

~Palindromic~

Connect: Create new connections. While this spell does nothing on it's own, it can be combined with Designate and Seek to create powerful wide reaching, or multi-target spells.
Potency has no effect on this spell.

Sustain: Continue and hold all affected spell effects. Without sustain, all spells occur and dissipate immediatly.
Potency determines the number of spells that can be sustained.

* Sustain is neccesary for buff and debuff spells to hold their effect.

* While Sustained spells remain active until the castor chooses to release them. Until that point the cards consumed by Sustained Spells cannot be refunded using satiety.

Transmute:Alter individual properties of a material, such as it's DT, PD, or special effects.
Transmutation can change special effects, but cannot add or remove them except on rare occasions.

* Transmute, by default, can only alter one propertie at a time.

Repeat: Repeat the effects of the targeted spells.
Potency determines how many spells may be attached.


Potency: The power of cast spells, potency has a variety of effects on the different base spells.
The most simple way to imagine the effect of potency is through the basic spell Generate(Force).
Potency 1: A light wafting.
Potency 2: A refreshing breeze.
Potency 3: A chilling gust of air.
Potency 4: A destabilizing gust of air.
Potency 5: A violent blast.

Potency is the first limiting factor when it comes to casting powerful spells, as a character can only cast spells up to a potency of their wisdom rank.
